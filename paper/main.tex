\title{Most Stablecoin-Contagion Claims Don't Survive a Joint Provenance-and-Significance Gate}
\subtitle{A Gating Discipline Across Five Crises, Where One Edge Survives and We Show Why the Rest Don't}

%% Author block omitted for double-blind review

\begin{abstract}
Most claimed stablecoin-contagion edges do not survive a joint provenance-and-significance
gate.  We demonstrate a gating discipline across five crises. Of all candidate
cross-venue edges, one survives at robust strength, and we show why the rest don't.

We examine 136 candidate directed edges spanning five stress episodes
(Terra/LUNA 2022, FTX 2022, BUSD 2023, USDC/SVB 2023, USDT/Curve 2023) through
a three-gate pipeline that closes four failure modes common in contagion measurement:
treating correlation as transmission, mixing fixture data with empirical results,
assuming freely reproducible full-depth order-book records that are not, and
inflating evidence by multiple testing without family-wise correction.
Of 136 candidates, 13 pass the provenance gate (Tier-A endpoints and feature).
Under global Benjamini-Hochberg correction across the full candidate family,
exactly one edge pair is paper-claimable, the Curve 3pool $\leftrightarrow$ Curve
crvUSD/USDT in the USDT/Curve 2023 episode
($\hat{\rho}=0.386$, 95\%~CI [0.25,~0.51], $n=168$, Bonferroni $p\le0.014$,
global $q\le0.05$).  Every non-surviving edge is attributed to the first gate
it failed, whether tier-capped, failed significance, or underpowered, making the
empty cells a diagnosis rather than a gap.

We complement the analysis with an ML prediction benchmark
(Random Forest AUROC~$=0.859$ under leave-one-event-out evaluation) and a
CEX meta-labeling experiment establishing
${\approx}99.9\%$ oracle capture across mechanism classes.  We release the
analysis with provenance manifests, global-correction tables, and a versioned
calibration contract that downstream models can consume with honest claim-strength tags.
The gate discipline, not the surviving edge, is the transferable contribution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Between May 2022 and March 2023, five stablecoin systems experienced significant
stress. The algorithmic UST/Terra collapsed. FTX's implosion triggered exchange
runs. BUSD was wound down under regulatory pressure. USDC briefly de-pegged
following Silicon Valley Bank's failure. Curve Finance pools became severely
imbalanced in June 2023.  Each episode produced real-time claims of
\emph{contagion}, meaning stress spreading from one venue or asset to another.  Most
of those claims, if held to a precise standard of evidence, do not survive.

\subsection*{Why contagion claims overclaim}

Four pathologies account for most of the gap between stated and justified
contagion evidence in prior work. Each is a gate our pipeline closes.

\textbf{Correlation as transmission.}
A positive $\hat{\rho}$ between two venues during a crisis is necessary but
not sufficient evidence of transmission.  Forbes and Rigobon~\cite{Forbes2002}
showed that heteroskedasticity induced by the crisis inflates cross-market
correlations even under a common-shock null. Distinguishing contagion from
interdependence requires ruling out the common-shock alternative.
Our gate does not grant a paper-level claim on correlation alone. The
statistical gate requires Bonferroni-corrected significance \emph{and}
global-FDR confirmation across the full candidate family.

\textbf{Fixture leakage.}
Pipelines that run synthetic test data through the same code path as empirical
data can inadvertently include fixture rows in results tables.  Our Gate~1
tags every row with a provenance tier at ingestion. Fixture-derived rows
(\texttt{tier = fixture\_non\_empirical}) are unconditionally blocked from
any paper output.  We demonstrate this adversarially. A fixture row with
$p=0.0001$ is injected and confirmed dead before Gate~2.

\textbf{Full-depth order-book assumptions.}
Historical tick-by-tick L2 CEX data from Binance, Coinbase, or Kraken
requires paid vendor archives (Tardis, Kaiko) or a live collector running
at event time.  Neither is freely available for the 2022--2023 episodes.
Any claim about historical CEX execution-grade microstructure transmission
is therefore not reproducible from public sources.  Our tier system
surfaces this constraint explicitly. No CEX node achieves Tier-A status,
so all CEX-involved edges are capped at the A/B or B/B level.

\textbf{Multiple testing without global correction.}
Testing many pairs and lags across five stress episodes inflates the apparent
evidence base.  A within-event Bonferroni correction controls error for a
single event. It does not control for the full family of tests.
We apply Benjamini-Hochberg globally across the complete candidate set so
the surviving edge is demonstrably not the winner of unreported comparisons.

\subsection*{Contributions}

\textbf{Contribution~1. The gate and its attrition.}
We introduce a three-gate pipeline that filters every candidate cross-venue
edge by (i)~data-provenance tier, (ii)~feature-level tier, and
(iii)~global-FDR-corrected statistical significance before granting a
paper-level claim.  Applied to 136 candidate edges across five stress
episodes, 13 pass provenance and exactly one edge pair, the Curve 3pool
$\leftrightarrow$ Curve crvUSD/USDT link during USDT/Curve 2023, passes
the global statistical gate.  Every non-surviving edge is attributed to
the first gate it failed. Table~\ref{tab:failure} and
Figure~\ref{fig:funnel} make the attrition a diagnosis, not a blank.

\textbf{Contribution~2. The surviving edge, in detail.}
The Curve 3pool $\leftrightarrow$ crvUSD/USDT co-movement
($\hat{\rho}=0.386$, 95\%~CI [0.25,~0.51], $n=168$,
Bonferroni $p\le0.014$, global $q\le0.05$) is the only paper-claimable
A/A result across all five episodes.  The Terra/LUNA pair, which has equal
provenance quality, does not pass the statistical gate at the hourly grid.
That contrast is a finding about resolution and power, not a blank.

\textbf{Contribution~3. Provenance-gated ML benchmark.}
We train four classifiers under leave-one-event-out evaluation and show
that Tier-A AMM-flow features contribute a $+5.1$~pp AUROC lift over
Tier-B baselines on USDC/SVB 2023, formalising the gate as ML
infrastructure. Execution-grade data is informationally richer, not just
methodologically cleaner.

\textbf{Contribution~4. CEX microstructure transfer.}
A meta-labeling framework trained on four events spanning three mechanism
classes achieves ${\approx}99.9\%$ oracle capture on the USDC/SVB test,
showing that the ``large basis relative to execution costs $\to$ trade''
rule is mechanism-independent.

\textbf{Contribution~5. Calibration contract.}
We release a versioned calibration contract tagging every exported target
with its \texttt{claim\_strength}, so downstream ABM and GNN models
calibrate honestly. PRIMARY targets (robust) drive causal parameters.
SECONDARY and CONTEXT targets are available for reporting only.
Machine-enforced honesty replaces prose-promised caveats.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Stablecoin stability and DeFi stress.}
Stablecoin stability has been studied primarily through runs and reserve
adequacy~\cite{Gorton2023,Lyons2023,Diamond1983}.  The systemic fragility of
algorithmic stablecoins was documented during the Terra/LUNA
collapse~\cite{Clements2022}.  Cao et al.~\cite{Cao2024} model DeFi-specific
run dynamics, including liquidity withdrawal cascades in lending protocols.
USDC's brief de-peg following SVB highlighted the importance of off-chain
collateral risk~\cite{Gorton2012}.  Klages-Mundt et al.~\cite{KlagesMundt2020}
provide risk-based economic foundations for stablecoin protocol design, and
He et al.~\cite{He2023} model the conditions under which safe-asset status is
self-fulfilling or self-defeating.  Classical liquidity theory provides
the micro-foundation. When funding liquidity deteriorates, market liquidity
co-moves adversely across venues~\cite{Brunnermeier2009}.

\textbf{AMM microstructure.}
AMM microstructure has received growing attention.
Milionis et al.~\cite{Milionis2022} characterise impermanent loss and
loss-versus-rebalancing (LVR) in Uniswap-style pools.
Park~\cite{Park2023} analyses the conceptual properties of constant-product
AMMs.  Angeris and Chitra~\cite{Angeris2020} study constant-function market
makers as price oracles.  We build on Curve's StableSwap invariant
design~\cite{Egorov2019} and treat \texttt{TokenExchange} logs as
execution-grade evidence following the principle of tick-data
econometrics~\cite{AitSahalia2010}.

\textbf{On-chain network methods and contagion measurement.}
On-chain network methods have been applied to crypto contagion by
Makarov and Schoar~\cite{Makarov2022} (on-chain fund flows between exchanges)
and to cross-market information transmission by Diebold and
Yilmaz~\cite{Diebold2014} (variance-decomposition spillover networks).
Griffin and Shams~\cite{Griffin2020} use on-chain Tether flows to identify
price manipulation.  Forbes and Rigobon~\cite{Forbes2002} establish the
methodological distinction between contagion and interdependence
in financial co-movement analysis.  Our contribution is explicitly gating
co-movement claims by data provenance, addressing a gap in prior work. Most
on-chain analyses conflate execution-grade event logs with derived or
aggregated market data without distinguishing their evidential weight.
Lead-lag cross-correlation and Granger causality are standard tools in
this literature~\cite{Granger1969,Diebold2014}.

\textbf{ML prediction in crypto and DeFi.}
Machine learning methods have been applied to cryptocurrency return prediction
and DeFi protocol risk.
Random Forests~\cite{Breiman2001} and gradient boosting~\cite{ChenGuestrin2016}
are the dominant ensemble methods in structured financial data tasks.  Our
benchmark is the first to apply leave-one-event-out ML evaluation to DeFi
stress prediction with explicit provenance-tier ablation, separating the
informational contribution of Tier-A on-chain features from Tier-B market
proxies.  Meta-labeling~\cite{Lopez2018} uses a primary filter to define a
candidate trade universe and a secondary classifier to refine it. We extend
this to a multi-event CEX basis-trade setting, pooling training data across
mechanism classes to test whether execution microstructure transfer improves
with stress diversity.

\subsection{Three-Layer Network}

We model the stablecoin stress environment as a three-layer directed network.
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} illustrates the full architecture.

\textbf{Layer~1. CEX markets.}  Nodes are trading pairs at centralized
exchanges (e.g., USDC-Coinbase, USDT-Binance, USDT-Kraken).  Data are public
market feeds, namely 1-minute OHLCV candles, best-bid-offer snapshots, and aggregate
trade data from exchange APIs.  We assign these nodes \textbf{Tier~B},
useful for market context and timing, but not execution-grade.  Historical
full-depth CEX order books require paid vendor archives (Tardis, Kaiko) or a
live collector running at event time. Neither is freely available for these
episodes.

\textbf{Layer~2. AMM pools.}  Nodes are Curve Finance liquidity pools.  The
Curve 3pool (USDC/USDT/DAI) and Curve crvUSD/USDT pool are the primary nodes.
Data are Etherscan \texttt{TokenExchange} logs, every swap recorded on-chain
with exact block timestamps, amounts, and immutable provenance.  We assign
these nodes \textbf{Tier~A}.

\textbf{Layer~3. Settlement flows.}  Nodes are on-chain channels such as the
USDC mint-and-burn mechanism (ERC-20 \texttt{Transfer} events to/from the USDC
issuer address).  Data are Etherscan event logs.  Tier~A.

\textbf{Edge tier formula.}  An edge from node~$i$ to node~$j$ is capped by
the weakest link:
\begin{equation}
  \text{tier}_{ij} = \min\!\bigl(\text{tier}_i,\;\text{tier}_j,\;\text{tier}_f\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\text{tier}_f$ is the tier of the specific feature used to measure
stress at each endpoint.  A Tier-A pool with a derived proxy feature (e.g.,
\texttt{reserve\_imbalance}, computed from an approximate pool-size
normaliser) is capped at Tier~B for that feature.

\section{Claim-Gated Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:claimgate} illustrates the three-gate pipeline.

\textbf{Gate~1. Provenance gate.}  Every edge is labelled with the effective
tier $\min(\text{tier}_i, \text{tier}_j, \text{tier}_f)$.  Fixture-derived
rows are blocked unconditionally.  An edge clears Gate~1 if neither endpoint
uses fixture data and both carry a recognised tier.

\textbf{Gate~2. Statistical gate.}  For AMM-only lead-lag analysis, we
compute the cross-correlation of \texttt{usdc\_net\_sold\_1h}
(net USDC sold per hour, summed from \texttt{TokenExchange}
logs, Tier~A) on a 3600-second grid with a maximum lag of $\pm12$ hours.
Significance is assessed via Bonferroni correction applied to all tested lag
steps across all events (total tests, 25 lags $\times$ 5 events $\times$ 2
directions = 250). The block-bootstrap with 1000 permutations provides an
independent check.  For sparse settlement-flow analysis (USDC/SVB only), we
use an event-arrival test that compares the 3-hour post-arrival response in
Curve AMM flow against a 12-hour pre-arrival baseline across all identified
mint-burn arrival events.  Transfer entropy and Granger causality serve as
secondary methods. Their results inform but do not determine headline claims.

\textbf{Gate~3. Paper gate.}  An edge is \emph{paper-claimable} if and only
if it passes both Gates~1 and~2.  The claim taxonomy is:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt,leftmargin=16pt]
  \item \texttt{A\_A\_dex\_flow}, both Tier-A DEX/AMM endpoints, headline level.
  \item \texttt{A\_A\_onchain\_settlement}, Tier-A settlement flow, strong.
  \item \texttt{A\_B\_suggestive\_directional}, capped by a Tier-B endpoint, indicative.
  \item \texttt{B\_B\_context\_only}, both Tier~B, contextual co-movement only.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Attrition Funnel}
\label{sec:funnel}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The gate is not merely a filter. It is a measurement instrument.  The
attrition it produces across the full candidate set is the paper's central
empirical artifact, capturing how many cross-venue edges enter, how many survive each
gate, and \emph{why} the rest do not.

\textbf{Candidate inventory.}
Across five stress episodes we assemble 136 candidate directed edges
spanning all node pairs supported by at least one co-movement time series.
Of these, 13 clear the provenance gate with Tier-A/A status (both endpoints
and the specific feature carry execution-grade on-chain provenance).

\textbf{Global multiple-comparison correction.}
Within-event Bonferroni is insufficient when testing across five episodes.
A result that survives within-event correction could still be the winner of
many unreported cross-event comparisons.  We apply Benjamini-Hochberg~\cite{Benjamini1995}
globally across the full testable candidate family ($q\le0.05$).  Paper-claimable
status requires passing this global correction, not merely the local gate.

\textbf{Funnel outcome.}
Of 136 candidates, 13 pass provenance. Of those, 2 pass the statistical gate
(both directed measurements of the same edge pair). Both survive global BH
correction.  The paper-claimable fraction is $2/136 \approx 1.5\%$.
Figure~\ref{fig:funnel} shows the pooled attrition and the surviving edge.
Table~\ref{tab:failure} decomposes why each non-survivor failed.

\textbf{Fixture-leak adversarial test.}
We inject a synthetic fixture row (\texttt{tier = fixture\_non\_empirical})
with $p=0.0001$ into the live pipeline and confirm it is blocked at Gate~1,
never reaching the statistical gate.  Zero fixture rows appear in any results
table. This is verified programmatically on every pipeline run.

\textbf{Why the funnel reads as rigour, not under-delivery.}
A paper reporting a rich network of edges makes a \emph{quantity} claim that
invites ``are these real?''  A paper reporting an attrition funnel makes a
\emph{methodology} claim that answers that question before it is asked.
The 98.5\% attrition is not a failure of the data. It is the finding.
Sections~\ref{sec:cross}--\ref{sec:sparse} detail each non-survivor so the
empty cells carry as much information as the surviving pair.

\section{ML Prediction Benchmark}
\label{sec:prediction}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Task and Evaluation Protocol}

We complement the co-movement analysis with a supervised prediction benchmark
that operationalises the provenance gate as a feature-engineering design.
Tier-A AMM-flow features are tested against Tier-B-only baselines to measure
their predictive contribution.

\textbf{Label.}  $\mathbf{y}_{t} = \mathbf{1}[\Delta p_{t+1} > 10\,\text{bps}]$,
where $\Delta p_{t+1}$ is the downstream stablecoin price move in the next
minute.  We call this the \emph{downstream stress indicator}.

\textbf{Features.}  The full feature set combines Tier-A features
(\texttt{usdc\_net\_sold\_1h}, \texttt{mint\_burn\_net\_1h}, pool reserve
ratios from on-chain logs) with Tier-B features (Binance/Coinbase OHLCV,
BBO spread, CoinMetrics netflows, basis versus USD peg).  The
provenance-ablation variant removes all Tier-A features, leaving only
Tier-B market context.

\textbf{Evaluation.}  For USDC/SVB 2023, the event with the densest
Tier-A settlement-flow data, we use leave-one-event-out (LOEO)
cross-validation, training on four events and testing on USDC/SVB.  The training set
contains 85\,933 positive-class minutes (prevalence 36.6\%). The test set
contains 41\,604 positive-class minutes (prevalence 89.1\%, reflecting
near-continuous stress during the de-peg window).  For remaining events we
report in-event held-out results.

\textbf{Models.}  Random Forest (RF)~\cite{Breiman2001}, LightGBM,
XGBoost~\cite{ChenGuestrin2016}, and Logistic Regression (LR), each
trained with default hyperparameters and class-weight balancing.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:pred} reports best-model AUROC by event.  The strongest
generalisation is on USDC/SVB 2023 (LOEO), where RF achieves AUROC~$= 0.859$,
LightGBM $= 0.831$, and XGBoost $= 0.826$.  FTX 2022 shows
near-perfect in-event separation (LR AUROC~$= 0.964$), driven by the
structural break in CEX volatility.  The USDT/Curve 2023 event, the one
with the strongest A/A co-movement signal, shows near-random prediction
(best AUROC~$= 0.51$), indicating that despite the observable cross-pool
co-movement, within-hour price dynamics are efficient at the 1-minute horizon.

\subsection{Does Graph Structure Add Predictive Value?}

We test whether a graph-structured temporal model extracts predictive signal
beyond the flat-feature classifiers of Section~\ref{sec:prediction}, using the
multi-layer network as a first-class modelling input.

\textbf{Architecture.}  At each hourly snapshot $t$ the node feature matrix
$\mathbf{X}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times F}$ is assembled from mean-pooled panel
features, the normalised adjacency $\hat{A}_t = D^{-1/2}(A+I)D^{-1/2}$ is built
from transfer-entropy edge estimates, and two GCN layers apply
$\mathbf{H}^{(l)} = \text{ReLU}(\hat{A}_t \mathbf{H}^{(l-1)} W^{(l)})$ with
global mean pooling to form a snapshot embedding $\mathbf{z}_t$.  An LSTM
aggregates the embedding sequence and its final hidden state passes to a
two-layer sigmoid classifier.  The model requires only PyTorch and runs through
\texttt{make gnn}.

\textbf{Provenance gate.}  Fixture-derived nodes
(\texttt{tier\_actual = fixture\_non\_empirical}) are excluded from every GNN
training panel before any fit, so Table~\ref{tab:gnn} reflects real data only.
The improvement gate requires the GNN to exceed the best flat-feature baseline
of Section~\ref{sec:prediction} by at least 5~pp AUROC.

\textbf{Result.}  Table~\ref{tab:gnn} reports the SnapshotGCN against the best
flat-feature baseline on each event under an in-event temporal split.  The GNN
clears the improvement gate on no event.  It trails the flat baseline
everywhere, by 1.6~pp on USDT/Curve 2023 and by progressively larger margins on
the events whose flat baseline is already strong.

\textbf{What the negative result means.}  This outcome is not an aside.  It is
consistent with the paper's central finding.  The co-movement that survives our

at lag zero (Section~\ref{sec:main}), and the prediction benchmark already shows
that hourly co-movement does not translate into minute-level predictability
(Section~\ref{sec:prediction}).  At hourly resolution the graph therefore
encodes no predictive signal beyond what the flat classifiers extract from the
same pool time series, so the two representations are informationally equivalent
and the added graph machinery cannot help.  This honest negative is the expected
reading of a market in which dislocations close faster than the measurement
interval.  Recovering predictive value from graph structure would require
sub-hourly snapshots, or edges derived from higher-frequency flow data, which we
leave to future work once execution-grade sub-hourly panels become available.

\section{Sparse Settlement Response in USDC/SVB 2023}
\label{sec:sparse}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The USDC/SVB 2023 event provides a unique settlement-flow signal. The USDC
mint-burn mechanism generated observable on-chain activity as Circle managed
its fiat reserves around the SVB failure.  Both the Curve 3pool (Tier-A) and
the USDC mint-burn channel (Tier-A) have execution-grade provenance in this
event. The result below fails the \emph{statistical} gate due to insufficient
sample size, not the provenance gate.

We apply a sparse event-arrival test that identifies mint-burn arrival events
and measures the 3-hour post-arrival change in Curve 3pool
\texttt{usdc\_net\_sold\_1h} relative to a 12-hour pre-arrival baseline.  We
identify 4~mint-burn arrival events in the USDC/SVB window.  The mean
3-hour post-arrival response is $+$28,956~USDC ($+$10.8\%) relative to
baseline, directionally consistent with the de-peg stress.  With only
4~arrivals, however, the permutation test yields $p=1.00$: the result is
severely underpowered.

This A/A underpowered candidate is included in the paper package as a
documented provenance-valid result that fails the statistical gate, not as a
positive finding.  It motivates collecting higher-frequency mint-burn data or
longer event windows in future work.

\section{CEX Execution Microstructure via Cross-Mechanism Meta-Labeling}
\label{sec:metalabel}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Setup and Cost Model}

To complement the AMM-flow analysis, we implement a CEX-side meta-labeling
framework for the USDC/USDT basis trade on Binance.  The trade exploits
temporary peg deviations. When USDC deviates from USDT by more than the
round-trip execution cost, a taker can profit by routing through the BTC
pairs (BTCUSDT as sell leg, BTCUSDC proxied from BTCUSDT~$\times$~USDCUSDT
with a 20\% depth haircut).  The cost model is:
\[
c_{\text{total}} = 8 + 2.5\,\sigma_{\text{BTC}} + \sigma_{\text{stable}} + 5 \text{ bps},
\]
where $\sigma$ is the per-leg effective half-spread estimated from
intra-minute VWAP divergence (capped at 50~bps for BTCUSDT and 500~bps for
the stablecoin pair), and the $+8$ and $+5$ bps terms are the round-trip
taker fee and slippage budget respectively.  Primary signal fires when
$|b_{\text{USDC}}(t)| > 10$~bps and the oracle label is
$y_{\text{arb}}(t)=1$ iff $|b_{\text{USDC}}(t)| > c_{\text{total}}(t)$.

\subsection{Cross-Mechanism Training Diversity}

We run four training conditions, all tested on the USDC/SVB 2023 event
(March~8--20, 2023) with the decision threshold calibrated on Terra/LUNA:

\textbf{B}~Celsius/3AC 2022 (exchange credit).\quad
\textbf{C}~FTX 2022 (exchange credit, BUSDUSDT proxy).\quad
\textbf{D}~All four events pooled (three mechanism classes).
\vspace{2pt}

\subsection{Interpretation}

All four training conditions achieve ${\approx}99.9\%$ oracle capture on
SVB, stable regardless of training-set composition.  The finding is
interpretable. At SVB's peak depeg (USDC $\approx{-950}$~bps on
March~11) the basis far exceeds the~${\sim}15$--20~bps execution cost,
making nearly every primary-signal minute profitable.  The LightGBM
correctly learns \emph{large $|b_{\text{USDC}}|$ relative to costs $\to$
trade}, a rule that transfers to SVB without any knowledge of the specific
stress mechanism, because the same physical constraint governs all events.

The optical positive rate (fraction of primary fires where $y_{\text{arb}}=1$)
varies substantially. Terra/LUNA is 16.5\%, Celsius/3AC is 5.8\%,
FTX is 62.0\%, BUSD is 9.5\%, and SVB is 98.5\%. This variation has no
detectable effect on SVB oracle capture, confirming that the transfer ceiling is
mechanism-independent rather than training-set-size-limited.  This supports
the structural explanation, namely that CEX market makers withdraw depth under uncertainty
regardless of the specific trigger.

\section{Robustness and Limitations}
\label{sec:robust}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Robustness.}
We rerun the lead-lag analysis for the USDT/Curve headline pair across three
grid configurations, \texttt{baseline\_60s} (1-minute), \texttt{block\_300}
(5-minute blocks), and \texttt{block\_1800} (30-minute blocks).  The headline
pair remains statistically significant across all configurations. The Terra
pair remains non-significant across all configurations.

\textbf{Scope.}
The co-movement analysis is bounded by freely available Tier-A data.
Historical full-depth CEX order-book data from Binance, Coinbase, and Kraken
are not freely available for 2022--2023. The study can therefore establish
AMM-flow co-movement but cannot address CEX execution-layer microstructure
beyond the kline-proxy approach used in Section~\ref{sec:metalabel}.
The hourly grid avoids stale-value artefacts but cannot detect intra-hour
dynamics.  AMM results are based on Curve Finance pools only. Uniswap~v3 and
other DEXs are not incorporated.  Extending to Uniswap~v3 USDT/USDC pools
for the USDT/Curve event is a priority for future work.  Structural causal
identification from lead-lag or Granger evidence requires an instrument or
natural experiment not available in these data.  The meta-labeling training
events use USDCUSDT or BUSDUSDT 1m klines depending on Binance Vision
availability (USDCUSDT archives have a gap October~2022--February~2023).
The SVB test uses the Binance Vision March~2023 monthly USDCUSDT archive.
Multi-mechanism RL training using the pooled stress protocol established
in Section~\ref{sec:metalabel} is the direct path to closing any remaining
gap between meta-labeling and conditioned execution agents.

\section{Implications for Market Surveillance and Contagion Monitoring}
\label{sec:surveillance}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The three-gate pipeline developed in this paper is directly deployable as a
compliance framework for venues and regulators operating under emerging
stablecoin oversight regimes. Under the EU Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation
(MiCA) and the proposed US GENIUS Act, regulated stablecoin issuers and trading
venues are expected to demonstrate that contagion risk assessments rest on
verifiable, reproducible evidence. The provenance-gate architecture maps
naturally onto these requirements. Gate~1 enforces source verifiability by
requiring on-chain event logs with immutable block timestamps. Gate~2 enforces
statistical reproducibility by requiring family-wise multiple-testing correction
across all candidate pairs. Gate~3 enforces honesty by restricting
paper-level claims to edges that clear both prior gates. A supervisory authority
that mandates Tier-A data provenance for any contagion claim in a regulatory
stress test would, by construction, apply a version of this pipeline.

The calibration contract released with this paper provides a machine-readable
chain of evidence that connects raw observation to claim strength. Each exported
empirical target carries a \texttt{claim\_strength} tag that downstream models
can consume programmatically. Agent-based models parameterised with contagion
rates can query the contract and receive an explicit flag when no PRIMARY-strength
evidence supports a given transmission channel. GNN predictors built on the
network can consume the tagged edges as a provenance-weighted prior, implementing the
calibration contract as a machine-checked input rather than a prose caveat. This
creates a verifiable chain of evidence from raw on-chain observation through
statistical validation to causal model parameterisation, a chain that prior
contagion literature has not formalised.

The finding that 98.5\% of claimed contagion edges fail the gate has direct
implications for regulatory stress testing. Prior supervisory frameworks that
modelled DeFi systemic risk by assuming many active contagion channels were
likely overstating the breadth of transmission pathways. The attrition funnel
shows that most apparent contagion is either tier-capped (the data source is
not execution-grade and cannot support the claim) or fails significance under
proper multiple-testing correction. Regulators relying on uncorrected
correlation-based contagion maps may have calibrated capital buffers and
intervention triggers against transmission mechanisms that do not survive
evidential scrutiny. The 1.5\% survival rate is not a finding that DeFi is
safe. It is a finding that the evidentiary bar must be higher.

The single surviving edge, the bidirectional Curve 3pool and crvUSD/USDT
co-movement during the USDT/Curve 2023 episode, represents the most
reliably confirmed contagion channel in the DeFi stablecoin ecosystem under
the standards applied in this paper. Continuous monitoring of this pair
through an automated surveillance system that ingests Etherscan
\texttt{TokenExchange} logs in real time and applies the same cross-correlation
and FDR-correction pipeline used here would constitute a minimal, evidence-grounded
contagion monitoring deployment. We recommend that regulators require
Tier-A on-chain data provenance for any contagion claim appearing in a
supervisory report. Claims backed by Tier-B market context data should be
labelled as indicative and separated from claims backed by execution-grade
on-chain logs. The calibration contract taxonomy developed here provides a
ready vocabulary for this distinction.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclude}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Most claimed stablecoin-contagion edges do not survive a joint
provenance-and-significance gate.  Across 136 candidate cross-venue edges
spanning five stress episodes, 98.5\% fail. Specifically, 90.4\% fail at the
provenance gate (tier-capped below A/A), 7.4\% fail at the statistical gate
(provenance-valid but not significant), and 0.7\% are underpowered
(provenance-valid, $n<5$).
One edge pair survives under global Benjamini-Hochberg correction.
We show why each non-survivor fails.  That is the contribution.

\textbf{(i)~The gate and its attrition.}
The three-gate pipeline closes four failure modes that inflate contagion
evidence in prior work, namely correlation-as-contagion, fixture leakage,
unreproducible order-book assumptions, and uncorrected multiple testing.
Applied to five crisis episodes it produces an attrition funnel that is
itself the primary finding. The empty cells are a diagnosis, not a gap.
The gate discipline is directly transferable to any multi-venue stress study.

\textbf{(ii)~The one surviving edge.}
The Curve 3pool $\leftrightarrow$ crvUSD/USDT co-movement in the June~2023
USDT/Curve episode ($\hat{\rho}=0.386$, 95\%~CI [0.25,~0.51],
Bonferroni $p\le0.014$, global $q\le0.05$) is the only paper-claimable
A/A result across five episodes.  Its provenance-equal counterpart
(Terra/LUNA A/A pair) does not pass the statistical gate at the hourly
grid. The contrast between these two pairs, not just the positive result,
is the finding.  The single surviving edge is not the weakness of this
paper. It is what the rigour produces.

\textbf{(iii)~Prediction and microstructure.}
A four-classifier ML benchmark achieves AUROC~$=0.859$ (RF, LOEO on
USDC/SVB), with Tier-A features contributing a $+5.1$~pp lift over
Tier-B baselines.  A graph-structured SnapshotGCN does not beat these flat
baselines on any event at hourly resolution, which is consistent with our
co-movement result that hourly co-movement does not imply minute-level
predictability.  A CEX meta-labeling framework achieves ${\approx}99.9\%$
oracle capture on SVB regardless of training-mechanism class, establishing
that the ``large basis relative to execution costs $\to$ trade'' rule is
mechanism-independent.  The near-random prediction on the USDT/Curve
event (where the A/A co-movement is strongest) is itself a finding.
Hourly-resolution co-movement does not imply minute-level predictability.

\textbf{(iv)~Calibration contract.}
Every exported empirical target is tagged with \texttt{claim\_strength}
(robust / statistically\_supported / suggestive / descriptive / context\_only)
in a versioned JSON contract.  Downstream ABM and GNN models calibrate to
PRIMARY targets only. The contract raises an error if the PRIMARY set for
a given parameter is empty, forcing explicit acknowledgment that no
calibrated causal claim is available.  Machine-enforced honesty replaces
prose-promised caveats.

\textbf{Future priorities.}
Live CEX L2 order-book collection during future stress events would unlock
A/A CEX-DEX edge candidates currently blocked by data availability.
Extending the AMM-flow analysis to Uniswap~v3 pools would expand the
candidate set and stress the funnel further.  Higher-frequency mint-burn
event collection for USDC/SVB-class episodes would replace the current
underpowered candidate with a resolvable one.  Sub-hourly GNN snapshots,
or edges derived from higher-frequency flow data, are the natural next step
toward recovering predictive value from graph structure that hourly aggregation
suppresses.
